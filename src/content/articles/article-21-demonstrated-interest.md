# Demonstrated Interest: Does It Actually Matter for International Students?

"Demonstrated interest" is one of those college admissions concepts that sounds more consequential than it usually is — but at specific schools, it matters enough that international students should understand exactly what it means and what's worth doing about it from thousands of miles away.

Here's the clear-eyed breakdown.

---

## What Is Demonstrated Interest?

Demonstrated interest (DI) refers to the signals an applicant sends that indicate genuine enthusiasm for a specific school. Admissions offices track these signals as one data point in evaluating how likely you are to actually enroll if admitted.

Why does this matter to schools? Because yield — the percentage of admitted students who choose to attend — is an important institutional metric. A school that admits 1,000 students and only 250 enroll looks different from one that admits 1,000 and 600 enroll. Admissions offices use demonstrated interest as a tool to predict and manage yield.

Tracked signals typically include: campus visits, virtual tour completions, attendance at information sessions or college fairs, interviews, email communication with admissions officers, and engagement with the school's admissions portal or events.

---

## Does Demonstrated Interest Affect International Students?

The honest answer is: it depends heavily on the school, and many schools don't consider it at all.

**Schools that explicitly state DI does not affect admission:** Harvard, MIT, Yale, Princeton, Stanford, and most highly selective universities are explicit that demonstrated interest plays no role in their admissions decisions. These schools receive so many qualified applications that they don't need to use DI as a filter.

**Schools where DI does matter:** A significant number of schools — particularly mid-tier liberal arts colleges and universities that are not oversubscribed with applications — do track demonstrated interest and factor it into decisions. These schools use DI as a signal that an admitted student will actually show up. For these schools, especially in borderline decisions, demonstrated interest can make a real difference.

**How to find out:** Check each school's Common Data Set (searchable online with the school name + "Common Data Set"). Section C7 lists factors in admission decisions, with ratings for each. If "Level of applicant's interest" is rated "Important" or "Very Important," demonstrated interest matters at that school.

---

## The International Student Challenge

The traditional signals of demonstrated interest — campus visits, in-person information sessions, alumni interviews on campus — are simply inaccessible for most international students. A student in Vietnam cannot fly to Vermont to tour a liberal arts college before applying. This is a genuine inequity in the system.

The good news: most schools that track demonstrated interest understand this barrier and give credit for the actions that are realistically accessible from abroad. The goal is not to replicate the in-person experience. It's to show that you've engaged seriously with the school in the ways that are actually available to you.

---

## What Actually Moves the Needle for International Students

**Attend virtual information sessions and admitted student events.** Many schools now offer virtual tours, info sessions, and Q&A events specifically designed for international applicants. Attendance at these events is often tracked. More importantly, showing up and engaging (asking questions, using your real name) signals genuine interest in a verifiable way.

**Email your regional admissions officer — thoughtfully.** Most schools assign regional admissions officers who cover specific countries or regions. Sending a brief, specific email to your admissions officer — introducing yourself, asking a question you genuinely couldn't find the answer to elsewhere, and noting something specific about the school that appeals to you — is tracked and remembered. Do not send a generic email. Do not email multiple times. One specific, genuine email is more valuable than three generic ones.

**Apply Early Action or Early Decision where it makes sense.** Applying early is one of the clearest signals of genuine interest — you're investing in this school before you have competing offers. For international students who are certain about a school and confident the aid will work, ED in particular sends an unambiguous signal. (Remember: ED is binding, so only apply if you're truly certain about both the school and the financial aid package.)

**Complete supplemental essays with genuine specificity.** "Why this school?" supplemental essays are a direct demonstration of interest — and the quality of your answer signals how seriously you've researched the school. Specific references to faculty, programs, courses, or campus culture you've learned about from virtual visits or research carry significantly more weight than generic statements about the school's reputation.

**Connect with current international students.** Many admissions offices maintain networks of current students willing to speak with prospective applicants from their country. Requesting and completing these conversations demonstrates interest and gives you material for your essays and interviews.

---

## What's Probably Not Worth Obsessing Over

**Following the school on social media.** It's not tracked in any meaningful way.

**Downloading promotional materials from the website.** No one is watching your downloads.

**Visiting for visiting's sake.** If you genuinely have the opportunity and resources to visit a campus before applying, a visit is valuable — for your own assessment of fit, and as a verifiable signal of interest. But manufacturing a visit solely for the DI check-mark, without genuine purpose, is not worth the cost.

---

## Putting It Together

For most highly selective schools, demonstrated interest is irrelevant — focus your energy elsewhere. For the mid-range schools where you're genuinely interested and where DI is tracked, a targeted set of real actions — virtual events, a thoughtful email, a specific supplemental essay — is worth doing once, done well.

The underlying principle is simple: schools want students who actually want to attend. If you genuinely want to go somewhere, let that show through real engagement. That's all demonstrated interest is asking for.
