# How to Read a US College Financial Aid Award Letter as an International Student

Your award letter arrived. It's got a lot of numbers on it, and at least some of them look encouraging. But before you celebrate — or despair — you need to understand what these numbers actually mean.

US college financial aid letters are notoriously inconsistent. Each school formats them differently, uses different terminology, and buries costs in different places. For international students who didn't grow up in the American higher education system, they can be genuinely confusing.

This guide will teach you to read any award letter clearly, calculate what you'll actually pay, and spot the red flags before you commit.

---

## The Three Types of "Aid" — and Why Only One Actually Helps You

Almost every financial aid letter contains a mix of three things. Knowing the difference is the most important skill you'll develop this month.

### 1. Grants and Scholarships — Free Money
Grants and scholarships do not need to be repaid. Ever. This is the only category that reduces what your family actually pays.

- **Institutional grants** come from the college's own endowment
- **Merit scholarships** are awarded based on academic achievement or other criteria
- **Need-based grants** are based on your demonstrated financial need

**This is the only number that matters when calculating your cost.**

### 2. Loans — Borrowed Money
Loans must be repaid, with interest, after you graduate (or leave school). They are not financial aid in any meaningful sense — they are debt offered to you as part of a package to make the total number look bigger.

As an international student, you are generally **not eligible for federal US student loans**, so any loans in your package will typically be private loans — often with higher interest rates and less favorable repayment terms.

Treat every loan in your award letter as a cost, not a benefit.

### 3. Work-Study — Earned Money
Work-study programs allow you to work part-time on campus to earn money toward your education costs. This is real money, but it comes with conditions: you have to earn it through labor, your hours are limited (typically 10–15 hours per week), and it is not guaranteed.

As an international student on an F-1 visa, you are only permitted to work on campus — and only up to 20 hours per week while classes are in session. Work-study can help, but don't count on it to cover a major gap.

## The Formula: How to Calculate Your Real Net Cost

Here is the only formula you need:

**Net Cost = Cost of Attendance (COA) − Grants and Scholarships Only**

Everything else — loans, work-study, any other category — does not reduce your cost. It shifts how you pay it.

**Example:**

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Cost of Attendance | $72,000 |
| Institutional Grant | $25,000 |
| Merit Scholarship | $8,000 |
| Federal Loan | $5,500 |
| Work-Study | $2,500 |
| **"Total Aid"** | **$41,000** |
| **Your Actual Net Cost** | **$72,000 − $33,000 = $39,000** |

Many schools present the $41,000 figure prominently. Your actual cost is $39,000. That's a meaningful difference — and across four years, that gap between "total aid" and "real aid" adds up fast.

---

## What "Cost of Attendance" Actually Covers

Every school publishes a Cost of Attendance (COA) that's meant to represent the full expense of attending for one academic year. It typically includes:

- **Tuition and fees** — the base academic cost
- **Room and board** — on-campus housing and a meal plan
- **Books and supplies** — an estimate, often $1,000–$1,500
- **Personal expenses** — an estimate for clothing, toiletries, entertainment
- **Transportation** — varies, but often underestimates international travel costs

For international students, the COA estimate **rarely accounts for:**
- The cost of flights home once or twice a year
- Health insurance (sometimes listed separately or as an opt-out)
- Visa fees and SEVIS fees
- Currency exchange costs and wire transfer fees

Always build a personal budget that adds these costs on top of the published COA.

## What "Meets Full Demonstrated Need" Means

You may see some schools advertised as meeting "100% of demonstrated financial need." This sounds like a promise to cover everything. It isn't quite that simple.

**"Demonstrated need"** is calculated by the school based on your family's financial information. The formula typically starts with your Cost of Attendance and subtracts your Expected Family Contribution (EFC) — what the school believes your family can pay.

Here's the issue: the school's calculation of what your family can afford may differ significantly from what your family can actually afford. And for international students, aid formulas vary widely by institution — some schools simply do not offer need-based aid to international students at all.

"Meets full demonstrated need" is a meaningful commitment when it comes from a school that (1) offers need-based aid to international students and (2) uses a generous formula to calculate need. It means much less from a school that calculates your need as $0.

## Red Flags to Watch For

When reviewing your award letters, be alert to these warning signs:

**Heavy loan packaging.** If a large portion of your "aid" consists of loans, your real grant is much smaller than the headline number suggests. Calculate net cost excluding loans every time.

**One-time grants.** Language like "first-year scholarship" or "freshman merit award" often signals that the grant won't renew in subsequent years. Ask explicitly: is this award available for all four years, and what are the renewal conditions?

**Vague renewal criteria.** Phrases like "renewable with satisfactory academic progress" can mean very different things at different schools. One school might require a 2.0 GPA; another might require a 3.5. Ask for the exact standard in writing.

**COA that excludes health insurance.** Some schools list health insurance as a separate mandatory fee not included in the COA. This can add $2,000–$3,500 per year to your actual costs.

**Gaps between aid and actual cost.** If your net cost after grants is still $45,000 per year and there's no explanation of how you're supposed to cover that, that's a structural gap that loans and work-study won't meaningfully close.

---

## Your Next Step

Once you've calculated your true net cost at each school, you're ready to make a real comparison. Don't compare schools by their total aid packages — compare them by what you'll actually pay.

And remember: you can ask for more. If one school's offer is significantly stronger, you can appeal to your preferred school with documentation of the competing offer. Schools do increase aid packages for admitted students who ask.
