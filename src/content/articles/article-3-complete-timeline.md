# The Complete Timeline: When International Students Should Start Applying to US Colleges

Most international students start thinking about US college applications too late. Not by a few weeks — by a year or two.

The students who get into strong schools with good financial aid don't start in 12th grade. They start building the foundation in 9th and 10th grade, before most of their peers have ever heard the words "Common App." By the time applications open, their grades are locked in, their test scores are ready, and their activity lists reflect years of genuine investment — not a last-minute sprint.

This is the timeline that actually works. It's not about stressing earlier. It's about making the right moves at the right time so you're not scrambling when it counts.

---

## Grades 9–10: Build the Foundation

You don't need to think about applications yet. You need to think about who you're becoming — because that's what applications will eventually reflect.

**Academics:**
- Take the most rigorous courses available at your school. US colleges, particularly selective ones, look at course difficulty, not just grades. An A in an easier class is worth less than a B+ in an advanced one.
- If your school offers IB, A-levels, AP, or other advanced programs, pursue the ones aligned with your genuine interests — not just the ones that look impressive.
- Protect your GPA now. It's much easier to maintain a 3.8 than to recover from a 3.4.

**Activities:**
- Explore broadly in 9th grade. Try things.
- By 10th grade, start going deeper on two or three things that genuinely matter to you. Depth beats breadth in US college applications.
- Look for leadership opportunities within your existing activities — you don't need to found a club; you need to show initiative and impact.

**Test prep:**
- Take the PSAT or a practice SAT/ACT in 10th grade to establish a baseline. Don't worry about scores yet — this is diagnostic.
- Begin light vocabulary and math review so you're not starting from scratch in 11th grade.

**College research:**
- Start casual awareness. When you hear a college name, look it up. Build a loose sense of the landscape.
- If you can attend an international college fair in your city, go.

## Grade 11, April–August: Research and Build Your List

This is the most underused window in the entire timeline. The summer before 12th grade is your most important research period — and most students waste it.

**April–June (Junior Spring):**
- Begin systematic school research. Don't just look at rankings. Look at financial aid policies for international students, specific programs in your area of interest, location, and institutional culture.
- Make a spreadsheet. Track: application deadlines, financial aid availability for international students, test requirements, application platform (Common App, Coalition, school-specific), and any early deadline options.
- Start following LilGrant and similar resources for curated information on which schools actually offer aid to international students.

**June–August (Summer Before Senior Year):**
- Retake the SAT or ACT if your score isn't where you want it. Most students benefit from one focused summer of prep.
- Draft your Common App personal statement. Your first draft will be bad. Write it anyway. You have all summer to make it good.
- Visit schools if possible — in person or virtually. Your impressions matter and will sharpen your final list.
- Finalize your college list: 2–3 reach schools, 3–4 match schools, 2–3 likely schools. Apply to enough schools to create real options.
- Request letters of recommendation from teachers before summer ends. Give teachers at least 2–3 months of lead time.

## Grade 11–12, September–November: Early Applications

The early round matters more for international students than most people realize. More spots are filled early; some schools are need-aware for regular decision but consider aid more generously early; and an early acceptance gives you months of breathing room.

**Know the difference:**
- **Early Decision (ED):** Binding commitment. If accepted, you must attend. Only apply ED if you're certain about the school AND the financial aid package works for your family — you won't be able to compare offers.
- **Early Action (EA):** Non-binding. You apply early and hear back early, but you keep all your options open until May 1. This is usually the right choice for international students who need to compare financial aid.
- **Restrictive Early Action (REA):** Non-binding like EA, but you cannot apply EA or ED to other private schools. Stanford, Yale, Harvard, and Princeton use this model.

**September–October:**
- Finalize and submit ED/EA applications. Common App opens September 1.
- Most ED/EA deadlines are November 1 or November 15.
- Request CSS Profile access — many schools require this for need-based aid, and it takes time to complete carefully.
- Continue drafting supplemental essays for Regular Decision schools.

**November–December:**
- ED/EA decisions typically arrive in mid-December.
- If you're admitted ED, confirm financial aid is workable before finalizing your commitment.
- If deferred or rejected, continue with Regular Decision plans — this is expected and normal.

---

## Grade 12, January: Regular Decision Applications

**January 1:** Most Regular Decision deadlines fall on January 1. This is not a soft deadline — submit by December 31 in your time zone to be safe.

**January:**
- Confirm all financial aid forms (CSS Profile, FAFSA if applicable, institution-specific forms) are submitted. Missing an aid form means missing aid.
- Your application is complete when the school confirms they have received everything: transcripts, test scores, recommendations, and essays.
- Take a breath. The hard part is done.

---

## Grade 12, March–May: Decisions, Deposits, and Visas

**March–April:**
- Regular Decision notifications arrive, typically mid-March through early April.
- Compare financial aid offers carefully — see our guide on reading award letters.
- Appeal aid offers if you have a competing offer from a comparable school.
- Attend admitted student events (in person or virtual) for your top choices.

**May 1 — National Decision Day:**
- Submit your enrollment deposit to your chosen school.
- Decline all other offers. This opens spots for students on waitlists.

**May–August:**
- Request your I-20 from your chosen school immediately after committing. Do not wait.
- Schedule your F-1 visa appointment as early as possible — wait times vary by country and can be weeks or months.
- Apply for on-campus housing. Housing priority is often first-come, first-served.
- Connect with your school's international student orientation program.

---

## A Note on Timing

Every year, students email us asking whether they're too late to start. If you're reading this in junior year (Grade 11), you're right on time. If you're in sophomore year, you're ahead. If you're in 9th grade, you have a real opportunity to do this thoughtfully and well.

The students who are "too late" are the ones who start in September of senior year with no test scores, no essay drafts, and no list. Don't be them.
