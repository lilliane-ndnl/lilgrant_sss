# What US College Recommendation Letters Actually Need to Say — A Guide for International Students and Their Teachers

Recommendation letters are one of the most misunderstood parts of the US college application — especially for international students and the teachers writing on their behalf.

In many countries, a "recommendation letter" is a formal document: a few polished sentences confirming that the student is hardworking and well-behaved, written in official language and stamped accordingly. In the US college application, this kind of letter is actively unhelpful. It reads as generic, tells admissions officers nothing distinctive, and quietly works against the student who submitted it.

This guide is for both students who need to ask for letters and teachers who need to write them — with practical guidance on what actually works.

---

## Why Recommendation Letters Matter in US Admissions

US colleges use holistic review, meaning they evaluate applicants as whole people — not just scores and grades. Recommendation letters are one of the few places in an application where someone other than the student speaks. They answer questions the application itself can't:

- How does this student engage in class — not just what grade did they get?
- What do they do when they don't know the answer?
- How do they treat other students?
- What makes this person distinctively interesting or promising?

A strong letter can move a borderline applicant off a waitlist. A weak letter — or a generic one — can leave an otherwise strong application feeling hollow.

---

## What Makes a US Recommendation Letter Strong

The single most important quality of a strong recommendation letter is **specificity**. Not length, not formal language, not prestigious vocabulary. Specificity.

An admissions officer who has read 3,000 letters can recognize a generic letter within the first two sentences. Words like "outstanding," "exceptional," "dedicated," and "hardworking" appear in almost every letter and carry almost no information. What carries information is a specific story.

**What a strong letter includes:**

**A concrete anecdote.** The best letters describe a specific moment — a class discussion, a paper, a question the student asked, a problem they worked through, a moment of intellectual initiative. This moment does more than any number of adjectives.

**Evidence of how the student thinks.** Admissions officers want to understand a student's intellectual character. Does this student ask interesting questions? Do they push back respectfully? Do they connect ideas across disciplines? Do they help other students understand? These qualities are visible in a classroom to an observant teacher.

**An honest assessment of where this student ranks.** Many countries have cultural norms against comparative statements or ranking students relative to peers. In US letters, a statement like "this is among the most intellectually curious students I've taught in fifteen years" carries significant weight. Teachers who are willing to make this kind of comparative assessment — when it's true — provide genuinely useful information.

**The student's voice and presence, not just their grades.** A letter that describes a student's work purely in terms of academic performance misses the point. What was this person like in the room?

---

## What Makes a Letter Weak (and How to Avoid It)

**Generic praise without evidence.** "Maria is an exceptional student who works diligently and contributes to class discussions" is a weak letter. It says nothing that distinguishes Maria from any other hardworking student. Every word must be earned with a specific example.

**Restating the student's resume.** If the letter simply lists accomplishments and activities already visible elsewhere in the application, it adds no value. Letters should offer new information — what the teacher saw from their specific vantage point.

**Excessive formality.** US recommendation letters are professional but personal. They are not legal documents or official attestations. They should sound like one thoughtful person describing another to a third party — conversational, warm, and direct.

**Vague enthusiasm.** Phrases like "I highly recommend this student without reservation" mean nothing if not supported by specific reasons. Support everything.

## A Guide for Students: How to Ask for a Recommendation Letter

Who you ask matters enormously. The right recommender is not necessarily the teacher who gave you the highest grade. It's the teacher who knows you best — who can speak to your intellectual character, your curiosity, your presence in the classroom.

**Choose teachers who:**
- Have taught you in a subject you care about, recently (typically 11th grade)
- Have seen you engage meaningfully — ask questions, push back, go beyond what was required
- Can speak to something specific about how you think or learn

**Give them what they need to write well.** When you ask a teacher for a recommendation, provide them with:
- A summary of why you're applying to US colleges and what you're hoping to study
- Your resume or activity list
- A brief note about moments in their class you found particularly meaningful — this jogs their memory and helps them write specifically
- The schools you're applying to and the deadlines involved
- Clear instructions for how to submit the letter (most US schools use an online portal)

**Give them enough time.** Ask at least 6–8 weeks before the earliest deadline. In many countries, teachers are not familiar with the US application cycle — explain the timeline clearly.

**Say thank you, specifically.** A handwritten note after letters are submitted is a small gesture that matters.

## A Note for Teachers: What US Admissions Officers Want to Read

If you're a teacher or school counselor writing US college recommendation letters, the following is worth knowing:

US admissions officers read your letter knowing you may not be familiar with American application norms. They will not penalize you for a letter that doesn't perfectly follow a US format. What they will notice is whether the letter is specific, honest, and personal.

You don't need to compare the student to American students or pretend familiarity with US academic systems. What you need to do is describe this student — specifically and honestly — from your direct experience of teaching them.

If you have taught this student for a year or more and they are genuinely exceptional, say so plainly. If they are a solid, hardworking student without a particular standout quality, an honest assessment of their strengths is more useful than manufactured enthusiasm.

**The counselor letter** is a separate and equally important document. In many international schools, this letter provides crucial context: explaining the grading system, describing the school's curriculum and academic rigor, indicating how this student compares to their peers, and flagging any circumstances that might be relevant to the application (family hardship, limited course availability, etc.). This contextual information is genuinely valuable to admissions officers and is often underutilized in letters from international schools.

---

## The Bottom Line

A recommendation letter's job is to make an admissions officer feel like they know the applicant a little better after reading it. The way to accomplish that is not through impressive language or formal structure. It's through specific, honest, personal description from someone who has actually watched this student learn.

For students: choose recommenders wisely, give them the tools to write well, and ask early.

For teachers: write specifically, draw on real moments, and don't be afraid to make honest comparative assessments when they're warranted.
