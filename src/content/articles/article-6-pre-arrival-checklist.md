# You Committed to a US College — Now What? What International Students Need to Prepare Before Arriving

May 1 is behind you. You paid the deposit. Congratulations — that decision took real work to get to.

Now begins a different kind of preparation: the logistical, practical work of actually getting yourself to the US and into your dorm room. Here's the big picture of what needs to happen between now and move-in day.

---

## 1. Your I-20 and Visa — Do This First

Before anything else, these two things are your actual priority. Everything else can be figured out when you land. Your visa cannot.

Request your I-20 from your school's international student office immediately after committing — you cannot apply for your F-1 visa without it. Then book your visa appointment as early as possible. In some countries, summer slots fill up weeks or months in advance, and if something goes wrong you'll want time to fix it.

---

## 2. Documents and Medical Records

Getting your paperwork in order before you leave is easy to underestimate and painful to fix from abroad. Beyond your passport and visa, think about medical records, vaccination history, and any prescription documentation — some schools require proof of certain vaccinations for enrollment, and US doctors will ask for records you might not have thought to bring.

If your documents are not in English, budget time and cost for translation before you leave.

---

## 3. What to Bring vs. What to Buy in the US

Most everyday supplies — bedding, toiletries, school materials — are readily available at US stores like Walmart and Target, often at reasonable prices. Shipping heavy or bulky items rarely makes sense.

What's worth bringing from home: items that are hard to find abroad (specific skincare, certain medications, medicated balms), sentimental items that make your room feel like yours, and anything you'd have to research extensively to replace.

---

## 4. Dorm Room Essentials

US dorm rooms are small and mostly unfurnished. You'll get a bed frame, a desk, a chair, and a closet. Storage, bedding, bathroom supplies, and anything that makes the space livable is on you. One thing that trips up almost every first-year international student: US dorm beds use **XL Twin** sizing — standard twin sheets from home will be too short.

---

## 5. Clothing for the Right Climate

What to pack depends entirely on where your school is. A student heading to Minnesota needs to think about winter before they arrive. A student going to Southern California does not. Research your school's climate across all four seasons, and leave room in your luggage — you'll accumulate things.

---

## 6. Medications

US pharmacies carry most medications but sometimes under different names, in different formulations, or requiring a prescription when your home country sells them over the counter. Bring a 2–3 month supply of what matters most to you while you get settled and find a doctor.

---

## 7. The Administrative Side

A few things that are easy to forget because they don't feel like "packing":

- Setting up a US bank account (many banks let you start this before you arrive)
- Getting a US phone plan sorted
- Registering for international student orientation, which is often separate from general orientation
- Understanding your school's health insurance enrollment or waiver deadline
- Connecting with other incoming international students — your class almost certainly has a group somewhere online, and someone has already figured out something you're about to struggle with
