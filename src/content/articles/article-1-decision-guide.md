# You Got Into Multiple US Colleges — Now What? A Decision Guide for International Students

Congratulations — you got in. Maybe to two schools. Maybe to five. Now comes the part nobody prepares you for: actually choosing.

For international students, this decision is more complex than it looks. You're not just comparing campuses and majors. You're comparing visa reliability, career pathways in a foreign country, financial packages denominated in a currency that isn't yours, and institutions that may or may not actually support students like you after graduation.

You have until **May 1 — National Decision Day** — to commit. Here's how to use that time well.

---

## Step 1: Understand What You're Actually Comparing

Before anything else, get every financial aid award letter in one place. Then strip out everything except this number:

**Net Cost = Cost of Attendance (COA) − Free Grant Aid**

That's it. Loans are not aid. Work-study is not aid. Any dollar you'll need to pay back or earn doesn't reduce your cost — it delays or offsets it.

Once you have the true net cost for each school, you have the foundation for every other comparison.

## Step 2: Ask These Questions Before You Commit

Most students commit without ever asking a school a direct question. Don't be most students.

Contact the financial aid office and international student office at each school and ask:

**About money:**
- Is this aid renewable for all four years? What are the conditions?
- If my family's financial situation changes, can I appeal?
- Are there any one-time grants in this package that won't be available next year?
- What fees are not included in the COA estimate?

**About visa:**
- What is your F-1 student visa support process?
- Do you have a Designated School Official (DSO) on staff and how accessible are they?
- What support do you offer for OPT and STEM OPT extensions?

**About life after graduation:**
- Do you have career services specifically for international students?
- What percentage of international students find employment or further study within six months of graduation?
- Do you have CPT/OPT advising built into your career center?

If a school can't answer these questions clearly, that's data too.

---

## Step 3: Evaluate the Things Money Can't Capture

Financial aid is the biggest factor, but it's not the only one. For international students especially, these four things matter:

**Visa reliability and institutional support.** A school's track record with international students — how they handle OPT delays, SEVIS issues, travel complications — varies enormously. Ask current international students, not just the admissions office.

**Geographic location and job markets.** A school in a major metro (New York, Boston, Chicago, the Bay Area, Seattle) gives you proximity to employers who actively recruit and sponsor international candidates. A school in a rural area may be academically excellent but harder to network from.

**Alumni network density in your target field.** If you want to work in finance, a school with 500 alumni in investment banking matters more than one with a great brochure. LinkedIn is your research tool here — search "[School Name] alumni" in your target industry.

**International student community.** This affects your practical life every day: housing leads, internship referrals, someone to call when your I-20 has a problem. Visit admitted student Discord servers and Facebook groups for each school before you decide.

---

## Step 4: Yes, You Can Negotiate Financial Aid

This is one of the most underused tools available to admitted students, and it works.

If you received a stronger financial aid offer from a comparable school, you can ask your preferred school to match or improve it. This is called a **financial aid appeal**, and it is a standard part of the process — not an awkward request.

Here's how to do it:

1. **Write a formal letter** to the financial aid office, addressed to a specific person if possible.
2. **State your situation clearly:** "I was admitted to [School A] and [School B]. I strongly prefer [School A], but School B's offer is [X amount] more generous. I am hoping you can review my package."
3. **Attach documentation:** Include the competing award letter as evidence. Schools need to see it.
4. **Be specific about the gap:** Don't say "I need more money." Say "The difference in net cost is $8,000 per year, which is significant for my family."
5. **Give them a timeline:** Let them know you're deciding before May 1.

You won't always get more. But you often will — and you'll never get it if you don't ask.

---

## Step 5: Why May 1 Is Non-Negotiable

National Decision Day is May 1. If you miss it without contacting the school:

- Your admission offer is typically rescinded automatically
- Any housing deposit you haven't paid will cost you your spot
- Financial aid packages at other schools may already be reallocated

Some schools have extended deadlines by a few days for international students in past years — but you cannot count on this. If you're waiting on a visa situation, financial information, or a scholarship decision, **contact the school directly and explain your situation**. They can sometimes grant short extensions for documented circumstances. They will not grant them if you just don't show up.

Once you've decided, here's what to do on or before May 1:
- Pay the enrollment deposit at your chosen school
- Formally decline your other offers (this is good manners and helps other students off waitlists)
- Begin your I-20 request process immediately

---

## The Bottom Line

You've done the hardest part. Now give this decision the same rigor you gave your applications.

Compare your real net costs. Ask the uncomfortable questions. Negotiate if the numbers don't work. And make your choice with confidence before May 1.
