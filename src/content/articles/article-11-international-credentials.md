# IB, A-Levels, CBSE, or Gaokao? How US Colleges Evaluate International Student Transcripts

One of the most common anxieties for international students applying to US colleges is a simple one: does my transcript even make sense to them?

The short answer is yes — US admissions offices read international transcripts regularly and have processes for evaluating them. But how they're evaluated, what converts well, and what you need to explain varies by curriculum. Here's a breakdown of the most common international systems and how US colleges typically approach them.

---

## The Core Principle: Rigor Within Your Context

US colleges don't expect international students to have taken AP classes or followed a US curriculum. What they evaluate is whether you took the most challenging courses available to you within your educational system, and whether you excelled in them.

The phrase admissions officers use is "rigor in context." A student at a school where the most advanced option is the national exam curriculum will be evaluated differently than a student at a school offering IB or A-Levels. Your job is to take the hardest path available to you — not to replicate someone else's path.

---

## International Baccalaureate (IB)

**Recognition:** IB is extremely well recognized at US colleges and universities. Admissions officers are generally comfortable reading IB transcripts and understand the grading scale (1–7) and the structure of the Diploma Programme.

**What carries weight:**
- Higher Level (HL) courses are viewed similarly to AP courses — they signal advanced academic rigor
- A strong predicted score (40+ out of 45) is competitive at selective schools
- The Extended Essay and Theory of Knowledge are viewed positively as evidence of academic depth and independent thinking

**College credit:** Many US schools offer credit or advanced placement for high IB scores, particularly on HL exams (scores of 5, 6, or 7). Policies vary by institution — check each school's IB credit policy directly.

**Tip:** If your school reports predicted grades, these are taken seriously in the application. A predicted 38 is meaningful information for an admissions officer even before your final scores arrive.

---

## A-Levels (UK Curriculum)

**Recognition:** A-Levels are very well understood at US colleges, particularly at selective institutions that regularly admit British and Commonwealth students.

**What carries weight:**
- A and A* grades (especially at A2 level) are viewed as strong indicators of academic ability
- The depth of subject specialization in A-Levels is appreciated — choosing 3–4 subjects and going deep in each is a legitimate academic path that US colleges recognize
- AS-Level results, where available, provide useful early data points

**College credit:** Some US schools award credit for A-Level results, particularly A and A* grades. Policies vary significantly — some schools are generous; others award none. Worth researching for your specific list.

**Note for applicants:** The narrow subject focus of A-Levels can sometimes raise questions in applications for programs that expect breadth (like some liberal arts programs). If your A-Level subjects are concentrated in one area and you're applying to a broad liberal arts college, your essays and application narrative can address how you're prepared for interdisciplinary study.

---

## CBSE and ISC (India)

**Recognition:** The Central Board of Secondary Education (CBSE) and Indian School Certificate (ISC) are well recognized at US colleges with significant Indian applicant pools — which includes most selective universities.

**What carries weight:**
- Class 12 board exam scores are the primary academic data point, alongside school grades
- Strong scores (90%+ aggregate, or high marks in your specific subjects) translate well
- Students who have taken additional competitive exams (JEE, NEET, olympiads) often include these as evidence of academic achievement beyond the standard curriculum

**Translation for US admissions:** Percentage-based grading is familiar to most US admissions offices, but it helps to contextualize your scores — note your school's average or rank if that information is available, since a 91% at a highly competitive school means something different than a 91% at one with lower averages.

**Common application path:** Many Indian students applying to US colleges also sit the SAT or ACT. Given the rigor of CBSE math in particular, Indian students often perform strongly on the math sections of standardized tests, which can strengthen an application.

---

## Gaokao (China)

**Recognition:** The Gaokao (National College Entrance Examination) is recognized as a rigorous national exam, but US colleges typically rely more on high school transcripts, teacher recommendations, and standardized testing (SAT/ACT/AP) than on Gaokao scores specifically. This is partly because Gaokao results aren't always submitted as part of an international application, and partly because US colleges use a holistic review that draws from multiple inputs.

**What carries weight for Chinese applicants:**
- High school GPA across years 10–12
- SAT/ACT scores (commonly submitted by Chinese applicants)
- AP exam scores, where taken
- Academic competition results (AMC, AIME, Physics Olympiad, etc.) are viewed positively

**Note:** Chinese students applying to US colleges often attend international schools in China that offer IB or AP curricula — in which case the IB and A-Level sections above are more directly applicable.

---

## Other Curricula: French Baccalauréat, German Abitur, and Beyond

US colleges with global recruiting see a wide range of national curricula and have processes for evaluating them. Generally:

- **French Baccalauréat:** Scores above 14/20 (Mention Bien or Très Bien) are competitive indicators. The rigor of the French system is well understood at selective US schools.
- **German Abitur:** A grade of 1.0–1.5 is considered very strong. US colleges understand the Abitur grading scale (1 = best, 6 = worst).
- **Other national systems:** If you're applying from a country with a less commonly seen curriculum, consider including a brief contextual note in your application — either in the additional information section or via your school counselor's recommendation — that explains how your grading system works and where you rank within it.

---

## Do You Need a Credential Evaluation?

For undergraduate admissions, most US colleges evaluate international transcripts themselves — you don't typically need a third-party credential evaluation service (like WES) for the application. These services are more commonly required for graduate programs or professional licensing.

However, if your school transcript is in a language other than English, you will need to submit a certified English translation alongside the original.

---

## The Most Important Thing You Can Do

Whatever curriculum you're in, the single most effective thing you can do is take the most rigorous path available at your school and perform well in it. US colleges are not looking for a specific transcript format — they're looking for evidence of intellectual ability, academic drive, and the capacity to succeed in a challenging university environment.

Your curriculum is your context. Make the most of it.
